Wasserstein GAN

Wasserstein Generative Adversarial Networks: Using the Wasserstein distance to improve GAN training stability and provide meaningful loss metrics

📄 ICML 2017 👥 Martin Arjovsky, Soumith Chintala, Léon Bottou 🏢 Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences, Facebook AI Research 📊 8,000+ Citations
📄 arXiv 📚 PMLR 💻 Code 🔬 Generative Models 🎯 Optimal Transport

Problem Statement

🔴 The Vanishing Gradient Problem

Original GANs use the Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence, which often leads to vanishing gradients when the discriminator becomes too good. When the real and generated distributions have little overlap, the discriminator can perfectly separate them, causing gradients to vanish and the generator to stop learning.

🔴 No Meaningful Loss Metric

The GAN objective doesn't correlate with sample quality. The generator loss oscillates without providing useful training signal, making it impossible to monitor training progress or detect mode collapse.

🔴 Mode Collapse

GANs often suffer from mode collapse where the generator learns to produce only a few types of samples instead of capturing the full diversity of the data distribution.

Core Insight: The Wasserstein distance (Earth Mover's Distance) provides a smoother metric for comparing probability distributions. Unlike KL or JS divergence, it can provide gradients even when distributions don't overlap, making training more stable.

Key Innovation

✅ Wasserstein Distance

The Wasserstein distance measures the minimum "work" needed to transport mass from one distribution to another. It's defined as:

W(Pr, Pg) = infγ∈Π(Pr,Pg) 𝔼(x,y)∼γ[‖x - y‖]

Wasserstein-1 Distance (Earth Mover's Distance)

✅ Kantorovich-Rubinstein Duality

Instead of directly computing the intractable infimum, WGAN uses the dual formulation which turns the problem into maximizing over 1-Lipschitz functions:

W(Pr, Pg) = sup‖f‖L≤1 𝔼x∼Pr[f(x)] - 𝔼x∼Pg[f(x)]

Kantorovich-Rubinstein Duality Formulation

✅ Lipschitz Constraint via Weight Clipping

To ensure the critic (renamed from discriminator) is 1-Lipschitz, WGAN clips the weights to a small hypercube [-c, c]. This simple constraint is sufficient for the theoretical guarantees.

Key Innovations

🎯 Wasserstein Distance

Replaced JS divergence with Earth Mover's Distance, providing smooth gradients even when distributions don't overlap. The distance measures the cost of transporting mass between distributions.

🔧 Critic vs Discriminator

Renamed the discriminator to "critic" - outputs a scalar score instead of probability. No sigmoid activation, enabling regression-style learning of the Wasserstein distance.

✂️ Weight Clipping

Simple but effective method to enforce 1-Lipschitz constraint: clip weights to [-c, c] after each gradient update. c = 0.01 typically provides good results.

🔄 Multiple Critic Updates

Update critic n_critic times (typically 5) before each generator update. Ensures the critic approximates the Wasserstein distance accurately before updating the generator.

📊 Meaningful Loss

The WGAN loss correlates with sample quality - unlike standard GANs, lower loss means better generated samples. Enables training monitoring and early stopping.

🚫 No Mode Collapse

Wasserstein distance provides gradients throughout training, preventing the mode collapse common in standard GANs where the generator learns limited sample diversity.

Experimental Results

WGAN was evaluated on LSUN bedrooms dataset and demonstrated superior training stability and sample quality compared to standard GANs.

Impact & Legacy

  • Foundation for WGAN-GP — WGAN with Gradient Penalty (Gulrajani et al., 2017) replaced weight clipping with a soft gradient penalty, improving training quality further.
  • Optimal Transport in ML — WGAN popularized optimal transport theory in machine learning, leading to numerous applications in generative modeling.
  • Stable GAN Training — Demonstrated that principled theory (Lipschitz constraints) can solve practical training instability problems.
  • Evaluation Metrics — Enabled meaningful training monitoring, which became crucial for large-scale GAN training in industry.
  • Spectral Normalization — Inspired alternative approaches to Lipschitz constraints, including spectral normalization (Miyato et al., 2018).
  • State-of-the-Art Models — Concepts from WGAN influenced StyleGAN, BigGAN, and other high-quality generative models.
